Calorie Calculator


What are calories?

Calories are a measurement of the energy a particular food or drink contains. Our bodies need a sufficient amount of this energy to function efficiently and to properly sustain our lifestyle we need to eat the correct number of calories. Different types of food contain different levels of energy, for example the three macronutrients contain a different number of calories. With fat containing double the number of calories in comparison to carbohydrates or protein. This doesn’t mean it’s not healthy just that it should be eaten in moderation.


Why is it helpful to count calories?

Keeping track of the number of calories you consume daily can be a helpful tool in achieving your goals, whether that be to lose weight or gain muscle mass. The recommended maintenance calories vary from person to person, depending on weight, height, gender, age, lifestyle, and activity level. Your maintenance calories are the number of calories you should be eating in order to maintain your weight in your current lifestyle. Based on your maintenance calories you can then calculate the number of calories you should eat that will allow you to effectively lose or gain weight sustainably. A general rule of thumb, is that a calorie deficit of 500 (500 calories less than your maintenance) can help you lose 1lb of fat a week. Although it is advised that any women should consume no less than 1200 calories and no man should consume less than 1500 calories. Counting your calories helps to indicate food habits which may need to be change and can help you hold yourself accountable, but it should be noted that the number of calories in a food doesn’t indicate the quality of the food or the number of nutrients in a food. Therefore it is equally important that when choosing food, to look at the overall nutrients that a food contains and not just the number of calories.

BMI

About BMI

The body mass index is a measure calculated from your height and weight, which indicates whether your weight is in the healthy range. For the majority of adults, an ideal BMI is in the range 18.5 to 24.9 range.

Interpretting your BMI

If you have a BMI:

  • Less than 18.5 you are in the underweight range. You may need to gain weight but should seek advice from your doctor or a dietitian.
  • In the range of 18.5 and 24.9 you’re considered to be in the healthy range of BMI. You should aim to maintain your weight.
  • In the range of 25 and 29.9 you’re considered to be in the overweight range and may need to lose some weight but should consult a doctor or dietitian before doing so.
  • Over 30 you’re in the obese range and may need to lose weight as your health is at a greater risk. Seek advice form your doctor or a dietitian.

Limitations of BMI

BMI only indicates whether a person is underweight or overweight, and should not be treated as a diagnostic. BMI doesn’t account for age, gender, ethnicity or muscle mass in individual however it can help identify areas where potential issues may arise. The higher the BMI, the higher the risk of different diseases such as diabetes, arthritis and high blood pressure. However BMI does not measure overall health or indicate the presence of any such disease. It is just a measure of size. It is not always a good indication of body fatness and further tests should be done before taking action.



Macronutrients

Protein

Protein is an important macronutrient that every cell in the body needs. It helps to build and repair cells and body tiddues including the skin, hair, nails, muscles, ligaments, tendons, and bone. Protein is also important for blood clotting, immune system responses, hormones , and enzymes. Protein is 4kCal/g and recommended intake is 1.6-2.5g/kg bodyweight. Good sources of protein are lean meat, fish, poulty, daily products, nuts, legumes and beans.


Fat

A small amount of fat (20-35 % of total caloric intake) is an essential part of a healthy body. Fat is a source of essential fatty acids, which the body cannot make itself. It also helps to absorb vitimans A,D, and E. Fats are the bodys main source of energy for endurance type activities and play an important role in hormone regulation. Fats are 9kCal/g. Good sources of fat include avacados, cheese, dark chocolate, whole eggs, fatty fish, nuts, vegetable oils.


Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the main source of energy in the body. They are sugars, starches, and dietry fibers that occur in plant foods and dairy products. Carbohydrates are 4kCal/g. The general recommendation for carbohydrate consumption is 45-65% of total caloric intake. Good sources of carbohydrates include bread, beans, pasta, rice, potatoes, corn, vegetables.
